# Archaeal Sm/SmAP proteins: a deep dive (2019–2026)

*Focused on the ~15 papers that specifically address archaeal Sm-like (SmAP/Lsm) proteins, set against the structural and evolutionary anchors they build on. Organized around the three questions that matter for a superfamily phylogeny: how the proteins are distributed across archaeal lineages, how their structures vary, and what the cross-domain comparisons actually establish.*

## The short version

Archaeal Sm/Lsm biology is a small, structurally-anchored, largely Euryarchaeota-and-Crenarchaeota literature carried by four or five groups. The recent decade has shifted it from "solve the crystal structure" toward "find the RNA targets in a living cell," but it has a conspicuous blind spot: essentially all functional and structural work is confined to cultured Euryarchaeota (haloarchaea, *Pyrococcus*, *Methanococcus*, *Archaeoglobus*) and thermoacidophilic Crenarchaeota (*Sulfolobus*). The DPANN and Asgard superphyla — the lineages that matter most for rooting the Sm fold and for eukaryogenesis arguments, are absent from the Sm-protein literature entirely. That absence is the single most exploitable gap for a phylogenetics program.

## Distribution across archaeal lineages

The only systematic census in the recent window is the Bonete group's bioinformatic survey, which examined Lsm proteins across 109 archaeal species and found that every genome encodes one to three Lsm paralogues, separable into two molecular-weight classes, and frequently sitting in conserved gene neighbourhoods next to Lrp/AsnC and MarR transcriptional regulators and ribosomal protein L37e ([Payá & Bonete 2023](https://doi.org/10.3390/microorganisms11051196)). That gene-synteny observation is a usable phylogenetic character in its own right. The taxonomic sampling in that survey, however, is dominated by well-sequenced Euryarchaeota and does not reach the DPANN radiation.

The functional-model organisms reinforce the same taxonomic skew. Recent primary work is concentrated in *Haloferax* (both *H. volcanii* and *H. mediterranei*, where the single Lsm forms homoheptamers and is tied to nitrogen and stress regulation — [Bonete group 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25010580); [Esclapez group 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biochi.2021.05.002)), *Pyrococcus furiosus* (where PfuSmAP1 binds poly(U)-rich targets co-transcriptionally and pulls down the exosome, RNA-modifying enzymes, and RNA polymerase subunits, [Grohmann group 2023](https://doi.org/10.1515/hsz-2023-0215)), and *Sulfolobus* (the SmAP1/SmAP2 paralogue pair, below). The foundational demonstration that an archaeal Lsm binds small RNAs at all came from *Haloferax volcanii*, which encodes a single Lsm1-subfamily protein co-transcribed with L37e ([Marchfelder group 2010](https://doi.org/10.1074/jbc.m110.118950)).

For your purposes the important negative result is explicit: a targeted search for Sm/Lsm proteins in DPANN or Asgard archaea returns nothing on-topic. Every DPANN hit in the literature is genome-resolved metagenomics, cell-surface biology, or metabolism — the RNA-chaperone machinery of these reduced-genome symbionts has not been characterized. Whether DPANN lineages retain, have lost, or have divergent Sm-fold proteins is an open, and directly addressable, question.

## Structural variation

The structural core of the field is stable and well-cited, which is an advantage for tree calibration. Archaeal Sm proteins were among the first to reveal the canonical Sm fold as a ring: the heptameric archaeal Sm structure ([Mura/Eisenberg 2001](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.091102298)), the demonstration that archaeal Sm proteins form both heptameric and hexameric complexes ([Törő/Suck 2002](https://doi.org/10.1016/s0022-2836%2802%2900406-0)), and the *Pyrococcus abyssi* Sm core with its bound RNA ([Suck group 2003](https://doi.org/10.1074/jbc.m207685200)). The "augmented" SmAP architecture — an extra C-terminal helix beyond the minimal Sm fold, was defined structurally in the same period ([Mura/Eisenberg 2003](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0538042100)).

Within the review window the structural work is dominated by the Nikulin/Tishchenko group at Pushchino, who have systematically compared crystal structures and U-binding modes across archaeal species: SmAP from *Halobacterium salinarum* in two forms with different RNA-binding ability ([Nikulin group 2019](https://doi.org/10.1107/s2053273319094476)), and a direct comparison of *Sulfolobus acidocaldarius* versus *Methanococcus vannielii* Lsm proteins that documented both similarity and difference in how the uridine-binding pocket is used ([Nikulin group 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biochi.2020.05.001)). This is the key point for structural phylogenetics: the fold is conserved but the RNA-contact residues and the heptamer-versus-hexamer preference vary between lineages, so structure-informed alignment will resolve relationships that raw sequence cannot.

Two recent items push structural understanding in new directions. The *Sulfolobus* SmAP heptamer is stable enough to serve as an engineering scaffold — a hybrid protein fused seven copies of the GroEL apical domain onto a *S. acidocaldarius* Sm ring and forced them to oligomerize, a proof of the ring's exceptional thermostability ([Balobanov group 2023](https://doi.org/10.3390/molecules28176196)), and denaturant/mutation studies have probed how the ring disassembles ([Semisotnov group 2022, disassembly study]). The distinctive archaeal feature throughout is paralogue multiplicity: unlike the single Hfq of most bacteria, crenarchaea such as *Sulfolobus* carry two SmAPs (SmAP1, SmAP2) that bind overlapping but distinct RNA pools ([Bläsi group 2015](https://doi.org/10.3390/life5021264); [Bläsi group 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/nar/gkx581)).

## Cross-domain comparisons

This is where archaeal Sm proteins earn their outsized conceptual importance, and the recent literature is fairly explicit about the claim. The consensus framing — stated directly in the Bonete papers, is that archaeal Lsm proteins probably represent the ancestral Lsm domain from which the seven distinct eukaryotic Sm proteins diversified by gene duplication, while bacterial Hfq is the functional analogue that arrived at RNA-chaperone activity on the same fold ([Bonete group 2023](https://doi.org/10.3390/microorganisms11051196); [Bonete group 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25010580)). The structural basis for treating all three as one superfamily was established by pairing the eukaryotic spliceosomal Sm cores ([Kambach/Nagai 1999](https://doi.org/10.1016/s0092-8674%2800%2980550-4)) with the archaeal Sm rings and, later, the bacterial Sm-like/Hfq structures ([Sauter 2003](https://doi.org/10.1093/nar/gkg480)); the synthesis that put Hfq and the ring-forming (L)Sm proteins in one comparative frame is [Weichenrieder 2014](https://doi.org/10.4161/rna.29144), and the crystal structure of an archaeal Hfq-*like* protein bridged the two branches ([Nielsen/Valentin-Hansen 2007](https://doi.org/10.1261/rna.689007)).

The functional cross-domain parallel is now supported by data rather than fold-homology alone: the *Pyrococcus* interactome showed archaeal SmAP embedded in the same RNA-homeostasis network (exosome, RNA-modifying enzymes) that eukaryotic Lsm complexes serve, rather than the sRNA-matchmaking role Hfq plays in bacteria ([Grohmann group 2023](https://doi.org/10.1515/hsz-2023-0215)). Broader evolutionary framing of archaeal RNA biology, including where Sm-like proteins sit relative to the unusual archaeal small-RNA repertoire, is reviewed in [Willkomm et al. 2018](https://doi.org/10.1042/etls20180034) and [Randau group 2018](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-genet-120417-031300). The narrative-review treatment specific to SmAPs remains [Mura 2016](https://doi.org/10.48550/arxiv.1606.03630).

## What this means for a superfamily phylogeny

Three concrete openings follow. First, taxon sampling is the field's weak point: no one has looked for Sm/Lsm proteins in DPANN or Asgard genomes, so a RefSeq-wide HMM search across those lineages would produce genuinely new distribution data and directly test whether the fold traces to the archaeal root or was lost in reduced-genome lineages. Second, the structural anchors are dense and well-resolved (heptamer, hexamer, augmented SmAP, single-copy euryarchaeal Lsm, paralogous crenarchaeal SmAP1/2), so a structure-guided alignment can be calibrated against known homologies rather than trusting sequence alignment of these short, divergent proteins. Third, paralogue history is unresolved: whether the crenarchaeal SmAP1/SmAP2 duplication is independent of the one-to-three Lsm copies seen across other archaea is exactly the kind of question a gene-tree/species-tree reconciliation would answer, and the Bonete census provides the starting copy-number matrix.

## Caveats

The archaeal Sm literature is small enough that this is close to a complete enumeration of the specialized papers, but several key methods papers are behind licenses that withhold abstracts, so a few structural details here are inferred from titles and the citation context rather than read directly from the abstract; the 2022 disassembly study was retrieved without a resolvable DOI in the sweep and is cited by description. Counts and distribution claims rest on the Bonete 109-genome survey, whose sampling is Euryarchaeota-heavy — the DPANN/Asgard gap is partly a sequencing-and-annotation gap, not only a research-attention gap, which is precisely why a fresh genomic search is worthwhile.
