# Hfq and Sm-like proteins in the Candidate Phyla Radiation: a literature survey

*The CPR/Patescibacteria companion to the archaeal Sm/Lsm survey. Organized around distribution, the genome-reduction baseline, and cross-domain framing. Each claim is marked **[read]** (from abstract or full text) or **[inferred]** (from title, citation context, or reasoning — not directly verified). The headline is a clean negative result.*

## State of the field: a near-total absence

**No study has examined Hfq or any Sm/Lsm-fold protein in CPR/Patescibacteria.** Across a twelve-query OpenAlex sweep (377 unique records, abstracts retrieved where licensed), not a single paper co-mentions a CPR lineage (Saccharibacteria/TM7, Parcubacteria/OD1, Microgenomates/OP11, Gracilibacteria, Absconditabacteria/SR1, Dojkabacteria, Katanobacteria, Berkelbacteria) together with Hfq, an Sm-like protein, or the Pfam families PF17209 / PF01423 **[read — confirmed by direct co-occurrence filtering and by targeted combined-term probes, all of which returned only generic oral-microbiome, metagenomics, or unrelated-taxon hits]**. Direct queries such as "Saccharibacteria Hfq" and "Patescibacteria Hfq gene" surface metatranscriptomics of the oral microbiome and ocean-microbiome surveys, none of which address the chaperone **[read]**. This is the CPR analogue of the DPANN/Asgard gap that anchored the archaeal study, except that in CPR the absence is even cleaner: there is not even an incidental annotation to build on.

Two structural facts about the field explain why. First, CPR biology is described almost entirely from metagenome-assembled genomes; the functional-genomics literature treats these organisms as obligate fermenters with minimal metabolic and regulatory repertoires **[read — Danczak et al. 2017 characterize CPR members as functionally uniform obligate fermenters](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40168-017-0331-1)**. Second, post-transcriptional regulation has simply not been a question anyone has asked of CPR: the CPR-specific RNA literature that exists concerns group I introns in tRNA genes, type IV secretion systems, and ribosome peculiarities, not sRNA-based regulation or its protein machinery **[inferred, from titles of the CPR RNA-related hits; abstracts were not all retrievable]**.

## The genome-reduction baseline

Because CPR itself is unexamined, the interpretive frame has to come from the better-studied reduced-genome bacteria, and here the literature is genuinely informative as a comparative baseline.

The foundational point is that Hfq is not universal and is a documented casualty of genome reduction. **[inferred]** The broader reduced-genome literature establishes the mechanism: in free-living prokaryotes with small genomes selection directly favours reduction, whereas in endosymbionts neutral loss dominates, producing very different retained gene sets from comparable genome sizes **[read — Martínez-Cano et al. 2015](https://doi.org/10.3389/fmicb.2014.00742)**. The extreme endosymbionts (*Carsonella*, *Nasuia*, and relatives, with genomes down to ~140 kb) are the reference points for what disappears first **[inferred, Moran & Bennett 2014](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-micro-091213-112901)**, and the Mollicutes/*Mycoplasma* reduction shows which RNA and translation machinery is retained at the ~1 Mb floor of autonomously replicating cells **[read, Grosjean et al. 2014 predict the minimal translation apparatus in Mollicutes](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1004363)**.

Critically for the CPR question, sRNA-based regulation does not necessarily vanish when Hfq does: conserved small RNAs are widely expressed even in a highly reduced symbiont genome **[read — Hansen & Degnan 2014 report widespread conserved sRNA expression in a small symbiont genome](https://doi.org/10.1038/ismej.2014.121)**, which means the presence of regulatory sRNAs in a CPR genome would not by itself imply an Hfq-dependent system. The endosymbiont gene-loss trajectory has been modelled as a mix of chance and necessity **[inferred, Sabater-Muñoz et al. 2017](https://doi.org/10.1038/ismej.2017.18)**. The precise fate of *hfq* across these reduction gradients, at what genome size and in which lineages it is lost versus retained, is stated in the specialist endosymbiont-genomics literature but was not directly readable in the abstracts retrieved here, so the specific claim that, e.g., *Buchnera* lacks Hfq is flagged **[to verify]** rather than asserted.

## Cross-domain and evolutionary framing

CPR has not been positioned relative to the Sm/Lsm/Hfq superfamily at all — there is no structural, phylogenetic, or functional work touching a CPR Sm-fold protein **[read, negative result from the superfamily-evolution and Sm-in-bacteria queries, which return the standard Hfq/Sm bacterial and archaeal players but no CPR taxon]**. The relevant scaffolding is external: the placement of CPR/Patescibacteria as a major bacterial radiation **[inferred, Hug et al. 2016, a new view of the tree of life](https://doi.org/10.1038/nmicrobiol.2016.48)**, the original description of CPR genomes and their unusual biology **[inferred, Brown et al. 2015](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature14486)**, and the MAG resource that expanded CPR sampling to thousands of genomes **[inferred, Parks et al. 2017](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-017-0012-7)**. Any superfamily phylogeny that wants to include or explicitly exclude CPR must generate the sequence data itself; it cannot cite a prior CPR Sm/Hfq study because none exists.

## The single most exploitable gap

**Whether CPR/Patescibacteria retain, have lost, or have divergent Hfq / Sm-fold proteins is completely unexamined, and — mirroring the archaeal finding, the reduced-genome, MAG-dominated nature of CPR means BLAST-based annotation will systematically under-report these short, fast-evolving proteins.** The archaeal survey's lesson transfers directly: DPANN/Asgard Sm/Lsm proteins were detectable only after completeness filtering, profile-HMM search, and ab initio gene prediction, with BLAST alone reporting false absence. CPR presents the same methodological trap with an even weaker prior (MAG fragmentation, no dedicated study, no incidental annotation). A profile-HMM search (PF17209 for Hfq, PF01423 for the broader Sm fold) against completeness-filtered CPR genomes, leveraging the high-quality Saccharibacteria oral isolates, which are among the few CPR lineages with cultured representatives and closed genomes, as the positive-control backbone where absence can actually be claimed, is a self-contained, publishable result: either the first report of Hfq/Sm proteins in CPR, or a rigorously-established true absence that parallels and contrasts with the archaeal retention finding.

## Reliability notes

The negative results here are strong because they were tested three ways: co-occurrence filtering on 377 records, targeted combined-term probes, and the superfamily-evolution queries. They are nonetheless bounded by OpenAlex coverage and by the fact that a genome paper can encode an Hfq annotation in a supplementary table without ever mentioning it in title or abstract — so "no literature examines Hfq in CPR" is well-supported, while "no CPR genome contains Hfq" is precisely the empirical question the proposed HMM search would settle, not a claim made here. Genome-size figures (~140 kb for the smallest endosymbionts, ~1 Mb Mollicutes floor) are cited from the reduction-baseline abstracts but the exact values should be confirmed against those sources **[to verify]**. No PDB IDs, residue numbers, or CPR-specific copy-number counts are stated because no source supports them. The reference table separates primary studies from reviews and marks the evidence basis for each.
