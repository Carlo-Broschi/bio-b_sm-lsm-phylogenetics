# Recent literature of Hfq and the Sm/Lsm superfamily (2019–2026)

*A landscape of ~615 works retrieved from OpenAlex across eight thematic queries, narrowed to 78 papers naming Hfq, Sm, or Lsm in the title. The map below reads that core set: where the effort concentrates, what has moved recently, and where the phylogenetic questions you work on remain thinly covered.*

## The shape of the field

The subfield produces a steady stream of roughly a dozen focused papers a year, but its center of mass is functional, not evolutionary. Structure/biophysics and sRNA-regulation/interactome work together account for more than half of the core literature; papers whose primary framing is evolution or phylogeny are a thin slice of four, and archaeal Sm/Lsm biology another three. This is the practical context for a phylogenetics program: the sequence and structural raw material is accumulating rapidly through functional studies, but comparatively few groups are reading it through an explicitly cross-domain evolutionary lens. That gap is an opportunity as much as an absence.

The functional work is anchored by a small number of high-output groups. Véronique Arluison's lab is the most prolific senior presence in the core set, concentrating on Hfq's amyloid-like self-assembly, membrane and DNA interactions, and biophysics; Sarah Woodson (RNA compaction and target search), Susan Gottesman and Gisela Storz (the foundational *E. coli* sRNA regulatory work), M.C. Wahl (structural work on Sm-like transcription factors), and María-José Bonete (haloarchaeal Lsm) recur as the other identifiable centers.

## Where the recent movement is

**Global interactome mapping has become the dominant method.** The most-cited recent primary work is the parallel RNA–RNA interactome of ProQ and Hfq, which showed the two chaperones bind overlapping but distinct RNA pools ([Melamed et al. 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.molcel.2019.10.022)). That CLASH/CLIP-style approach has since been applied across species and conditions — Hfq-licensed interactomes under nitrogen starvation in *E. coli* ([Wigneshweraraj group 2023](https://doi.org/10.1093/nar/gkad1211)), the core-versus-accessory Hfq interactome across *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* strains ([Gebhardt et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-022-28849-w)), and transcriptome-wide RNA-structurome reprogramming ([Deng group 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2024.114544)). The through-line is that Hfq's regulon is now defined empirically and comparatively, which is exactly the kind of data a phylogenetic study can overlay onto a species tree.

**Hfq biophysics has expanded well beyond the sRNA-matchmaker picture.** Recent work frames Hfq as undergoing heterotypic phase separation tied to its chaperone role ([Amster-Choder group 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2022.111881)), forming lipid-driven amyloid assemblies, and actively compacting RNA to accelerate target search ([Woodson group 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-024-46316-6)). The C-terminal and lateral surfaces — long treated as accessory, are increasingly the focus; a 2025 transcriptome-scale study identified conserved residues in the poorly-understood lateral rim that shape target selection ([Wigneshweraraj group 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nar/gkaf019)).

**The Sm/Lsm connection to Hfq is being actively redrawn.** Two recent items bear directly on superfamily evolution. A 2025 study dissected the interconversion between Lsm-type and Sm-type hetero-oligomeric rings and the mechanisms that toggle between them ([Zhang group 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nar/gkaf451)), and a structural/functional paper showed the Sm-like protein Rof inhibiting the transcription-termination factor Rho ([Wahl group 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-024-47439-6)) — a reminder that Sm-fold proteins in bacteria do jobs unrelated to sRNA matchmaking. A 2025 report that *Tevenvirinae* phages encode their own family of LSm-like fold proteins ([Hinton group 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.csbj.2025.05.028)) extends the superfamily's known distribution into phage genomes, which is relevant to any argument about horizontal transfer of the fold.

## The evolutionary and cross-domain thread

This is the part of the field closest to your work, and it is carried by a handful of papers rather than a dense literature. The conceptual foundation predates the review window: the Sm core-domain crystal structure established the shared fold ([Törő et al. 2001](https://doi.org/10.1093/emboj/20.9.2293)), archaeal Sm proteins were shown to form both heptameric and hexameric rings ([Törő et al. 2002](https://doi.org/10.1016/s0022-2836%2802%2900406-0)), and the explicit superfamily-evolution synthesis — mapping Sm/Lsm genes across the three domains and arguing for their deep ancestry, remains [Veretnik, Wodak & Bourne 2009](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pcbi.1000315). Within the recent window, the cross-domain structural bridge is the *Caulobacter crescentus* Hfq structure, which revealed features conserved with the eukaryotic/archaeal Sm architecture ([Hardwick group 2019](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1814428116)).

The genuinely recent evolutionary contributions are concentrated in the archaeal Sm/Lsm literature and are largely the work of the Bonete group in the haloarchaea: a comprehensive bioinformatic survey of Lsm biodiversity ([2023, Microorganisms](https://doi.org/10.3390/microorganisms11051196)) and an analysis of Lsm-mediated regulation in *Haloferax* ([2024, IJMS](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25010580)). Crenarchaeal SmAP1/SmAP2 heptamers had been characterized earlier ([Bläsi group 2015](https://doi.org/10.3390/life5021264)). On the eukaryotic side, the Lsm1-7-Pat1 assembly architecture ([Conti group 2013](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2013.10.004)) remains the structural reference for the cytoplasmic decay ring. What is conspicuously absent from the recent record is a large-scale, sequence-based maximum-likelihood phylogeny of the whole Hfq/Sm/Lsm superfamily sampled evenly across bacteria, archaea, and eukaryotes — the modern-methods reanalysis that the accumulated interactome and structural data now make feasible.

## Reading the map for your program

Three things follow for a molecular-phylogenetics project. First, the comparative interactome datasets (Pseudomonas core/accessory, the ProQ/Hfq partition, the phage LSm-like family) are ready-made character data for mapping functional divergence onto a tree — the empirical work has been done, the evolutionary synthesis has not. Second, the superfamily's structural anchors are stable and well-cited, so a phylogeny can be calibrated against known fold homologies (Sm core, archaeal heptamer/hexamer, bacterial Hfq hexamer) rather than sequence alone, useful given how divergent these short proteins are at the sequence level. Third, the single most-cited evolutionary synthesis in the field is now over fifteen years old; an IQ-TREE/MrBayes reanalysis with current RefSeq sampling and current models would be filling a real and citable gap, not duplicating recent work.

## Caveats on this map

The counts come from a bounded keyword sweep of OpenAlex (eight core queries plus targeted evolution/archaea searches and one round of citation-graph expansion), capped per query, so absolute numbers undercount the true literature and the most recent months are under-indexed — the map is reliable for *relative* emphasis and for identifying anchor papers, not as an exhaustive census. Papers were assigned to a single theme by title keywords, so a few multi-topic works sit in one bucket when they span several. The full retrieved set is in the attached tables for you to re-slice.
